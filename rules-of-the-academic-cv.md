# Chapter 24: Rules of the Academic CV

## Overview

This chapter provides detailed guidance on creating an effective academic CV following American conventions, primarily geared toward humanities and social sciences. Kelsky emphasizes that while the CV genre allows variation, following these rules produces a highly readable, well-organized document. She notes that candidates should always verify conventions with experts in their specific field.

## 1. General Formatting Rules

**Layout essentials:**
- One-inch margins on all sides
- 12-point type throughout (candidate's name can be 14-16 point)
- Single-spaced
- Headings in bold and all caps; subheadings in bold only
- Italics reserved exclusively for journal and book titles
- All elements left-justified

**Critical prohibitions:**
- **No bullet points ever** — "This is not a résumé"
- No box or column formatting (interferes with ongoing updates)
- No "cont'd" headings at page tops
- No narrative verbiage anywhere
- No descriptions of duties, paragraphs about publications, or explanations of grants
- No personal stories or self-indulgent ruminations

**The Year-to-the-Left Rule:** Years should be left-justified with tabs separating them from entry content. This allows readers to quickly assess productivity over time—"when you produced is as important as what you produced."

## 2. Heading Material

- Name centered at top (14-16 point)
- "Curriculum Vitae" below name (traditional but going out of style—check with advisor)
- Date (optional; senior scholars always date their CVs)
- Institutional and home addresses on left and right sides respectively

## 3. Content Sections (in order)

### Education
- Always listed first, by degree (not institution)
- Use abbreviations (Ph.D., M.A., B.A.), not spelled out
- Include department, institution, year of completion
- No starting dates
- Dissertation title/advisor only if ABD or recently finished

### Professional Appointments
- Contract positions only: tenure-track, instructorships, visiting positions, postdocs
- Not adjunct gigs, teaching assistantships, or courses taught as adjunct
- Include institution, department, title, and years

### Publications
Subdivided by type:
- Books
- Edited Volumes
- Refereed Journal Articles
- Book Chapters
- Conference Proceedings
- Encyclopedia Entries
- Book Reviews
- Manuscripts in Submission (include journal title)
- Manuscripts in Preparation
- Web-Based Publications
- Other Publications

Forthcoming accepted publications go at the top with "in press" or "forthcoming."

### Awards and Honors
Year at left, reverse chronological order. Dollar amounts are field-specific.

### Grants and Fellowships
Funder, institutional location, and year. Dollar amounts field-specific.

### Invited Talks
Talks at other campuses (not your own). Title, institution, year at left.

### Conference Activity
Subheadings: Conferences Organized, Panels Organized, Papers Presented, Discussant. Future accepted conferences listed with future dates.

### Campus/Departmental Talks
Talks on your own campus—lower status than invited talks.

### Teaching Experience
- Subdivide by institution, area, or graduate/undergraduate
- List course titles (not numbers) with terms/years in parentheses
- Condense if exceeding ~15 courses
- No narrative or "duties"
- TA experience goes here

### Research Experience
For research assistant and lab experience. One detailed sentence allowed for complex team projects.

### Service to Profession
Journal reviewing, professional organization leadership. Entry left-justified with year embedded (exception to year-to-the-left rule).

### Departmental/University Service
Committee work, faculty senate. Same formatting as above.

### Optional Sections
- Extra Training (common for UK/European candidates)
- Community Involvement/Outreach
- Media Coverage (no blurbs—"These are not movie trailers")
- Related Professional Skills
- Nonacademic Work (only if relevant)
- Teaching Areas/Courses Prepared to Teach (max ~8 courses)
- Languages (with proficiency levels clearly marked)
- Professional Memberships

### References
- Listed vertically with full name, title, and contact information
- No "Dr." or "Professor" prefixes
- No explanatory narrative except one "Teaching Reference" designation

## The Central Organizing Principle: Peer Review

The CV should be organized according to the **Principle of Peer Review**: peer-reviewed and competitive items take precedence over those that are not. The hierarchy reflects:

1. Publications (peer-reviewed highest)
2. Awards, honors, fellowships, grants (competitive)
3. Invited talks (individual recognition)
4. Conference papers (volunteered)
5. Campus talks (less competitive)
6. Teaching (not inherently competitive)
7. Voluntary training (noncompetitive)

## Special Note on Teaching-Focused Jobs

When applying to teaching-centric positions, teaching content may be moved higher (after Education), but research content should never be removed—teaching-focused institutions increasingly want evidence of research productivity.
